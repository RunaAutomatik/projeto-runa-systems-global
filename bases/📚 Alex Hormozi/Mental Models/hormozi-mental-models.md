# Hormozi Mental Models

A collection of Hormozi's distinct thinking frameworks — each named, explained, and quoted.

---

## 1. Sales as Transference of Conviction

**Model:** Sales is not about manipulation or technique. It is the transfer of belief from seller to buyer, conducted over a bridge of trust.

> "Sales is a transference of conviction from you to them through a bridge of trust."

If the salesperson doesn't believe in the product, there is no conviction to transfer — no matter how good the script. Conviction precedes technique. This is why:
- The best salespeople come from former customers
- A salesperson who starts losing momentum has lost conviction, not skill
- Training cannot fix disbelief — only restored conviction can

**Application:** Before optimizing the script, ensure belief. Actively maintain conviction through testimonials, results tracking, and regular customer success exposure.

---

## 2. The Belief-Trait-Skill Trident

**Model:** Every time someone is stuck, it's because of one or more of three deficiencies:

1. **Belief** — wrong or missing belief about what's possible
2. **Trait** — character quality that needs development
3. **Skill** — technical know-how that needs to be acquired

> "When I'm looking at a business or business owner who's stuck at a certain level, I ask: is this a belief problem, a trait problem, or a skill problem? You need all three."

People who focus only on character talk only about traits. People who focus only on execution talk only about skills. People in "woo" spaces talk only about beliefs. The complete system requires all three.

**Application for belief:** When someone says "no one in my market makes more than $2M/month," the question is: "If all your competitors combined do $20M, why couldn't one person do all of it?"

---

## 3. The Rocking Chair Close

**Model:** When a prospect says "I need to think about it," they are not actually going to go home and carefully deliberate. They will get overwhelmed by life and never return.

> "Let's be real — you're not going to go back home, sit in your rocking chair on your deck, and think 'hmm, what am I going to do?' You're going to go home, you're going to realize you have to buy groceries, cook, pick up the kids. And three weeks from now when we're already full, you'll say 'oh shoot, I should have done that thing. It's too late now.'"

The close: "In that moment, you will have made the decision. So we might as well confront it now, with me right here and all the information at your fingertips."

**Application:** Reframe "I need to think about it" as a decision already being made by inaction. The question is not whether they decide — it's whether they decide with full information or without.

---

## 4. "Fair Enough" — The Reciprocal Concession Close

**Model:** When you make a concession, humans feel psychologically compelled to reciprocate. Even if they don't like you, the mechanism still triggers.

> "My favorite close is 'fair enough.' You make a concession, then you say 'fair enough.' It's very hard for someone to deny this request because they feel like they need to reciprocate."

Usage pattern:
- Prospect: "That's a lot."
- You: "No worries. What if we did [concession]? Fair enough?"
- Prospect: "Well, he made a concession — that sounds fair."
- Then move to close.

Related — the low-pressure close question:
> "Would it be totally crazy for us to move forward today?"
or
> "Would you be totally opposed to moving forward?"

This framing makes the prospect feel safe saying yes because the negative framing ("opposed to") requires them to actively object rather than actively agree.

---

## 5. The Reciprocity Weapon

**Model:** Giving first triggers a biological compulsion to return the favor — even toward people the recipient doesn't like.

> "In a study, people handed strangers a Coke before asking them to buy raffle tickets for charity. Those who got a Coke bought twice as many tickets. And when researchers ran the study again — even people who didn't like the person who gave them the Coke bought twice as many tickets. Reciprocity is so strong it compels people to buy from people they don't like."

**Application:** Give value, content, information, or a small gift before asking for a sale. This is also why Hormozi gives away books at near-zero cost.

---

## 6. What Boat Are You In? (Market Selection Model)

**Model:** Effort and skill matter, but the market you're in matters more. Choosing the right market is the highest-leverage decision in business.

> "It is far more important what boat you are in than how hard you row." — Warren Buffett (cited by Hormozi)

> "Great entrepreneur in bad market — market wins. Bad entrepreneur in great market — market wins."

The wealthiest people in history largely made their money during specific windows of arbitrage — periods where disproportionate return was available. The skill of the entrepreneur mattered less than recognizing and entering the right window.

**Application:** Build a full stack of skills so that when an arbitrage window opens, you can go all in.

## 7. The Knowledge = Time Compression Model

**Model:** Buying knowledge is the only way to buy time. Every dollar spent on education purchases the equivalent of years of trial-and-error compressed into weeks.

> "When you buy knowledge, you buy time. Learning is the only way to pay down the time tax of ignorance."

> "The question is not whether you're going to buy [the knowledge] or not — it's how do you want to pay? Money, or time? Do you want it to take 12 weeks or 12 years?"

Human civilization advances this way: Edison spent years on the lightbulb; everyone else just bought the lesson. The wealthy buy other people's mistakes because it's the only available lever for compressing time.

**Application:** Frame coaching/programs not as a cost but as a time purchase. The ROI is not what you get — it's the years you don't waste.

## 8. The CLOSER Framework

**Model:** A 6-step sales conversation architecture that guides a prospect from arrival to commitment.

| Letter | Step | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **C** | Clarify | Why are they there? What made them reach out? |
| **L** | Label | Identify the problem you plan to solve for them |
| **O** | Overview | Surface their past experiences and failed attempts to solve this |
| **S** | Sell the vacation | Sell the experience of the result, not the mechanism to get there |
| **E** | Explain their concerns | Address hesitations to move them toward commitment |
| **R** | Reinforce | Affirm their decision; start the relationship on the right foot |

> "Sell them what they're going to experience — what it's going to be like when they experience the result — not the way you're going to get them there."

## 9. Activity vs. Progress Trap

**Model:** Most people mistake movement for advancement. Being busy is not the same as being effective.

> "People mistake activity for progress."

The key is to track leading indicators — actions that causally produce outcomes — not lagging indicators (revenue, profit), which only confirm past decisions.

**Leading indicator logic:** If you know that 10 calls produce 1 sale and you want 5 sales, you need 50 calls. Track calls, not sales. Sales is the lagging echo of the calls you made last month.

---

## 10. The Common Factor Analysis (Pareto Client Model)

**Model:** Instead of trying to get more of all clients, identify the characteristics shared by your best 20% (who likely generate 80% of revenue) — then only sell to that type.

> "Take 100 clients. Look at the top 20. What do they all have in common? Now imagine if all 100 were that 20% type — you'd 5x your revenue with the same operational drag."

**Application:** Run a common factor analysis on your best clients before spending on acquisition. The avatar you discover will reshape your offer, messaging, and channel selection.

---

## 11. Piggyback on Existing Habits

**Model:** Changing human behavior is extremely hard. The path of least resistance is to attach new behaviors to habits already in place.

> "Getting someone to make a new habit is incredibly difficult. So just piggyback on habits they already have. If I need you to take a supplement, I want you to put it next to your toothbrush — so we don't have to make a new habit, we just associate usage with something they're already doing."

**Application in sales:** When explaining product adoption, tie the new action to an existing trigger in the customer's daily routine.

---

## 12. The Virtuous vs. Vicious Price Cycle

**Model:** Price is a lever that creates self-reinforcing feedback loops in either direction.

**Virtuous (high price):**
Higher price → more emotional investment → higher perceived value → better results → less demanding customer → more profit for reinvestment → higher conviction → higher close rate

**Vicious (low price):**
Lower price → less investment → lower perceived value → worse results → more demanding customers → less profit → less conviction → lower close rate

> "Your cheapest customers are the worst people. The ones who pay you the most are the easiest to deal with."

---

## 13. Success is Colorblind

**Model:** Success rewards action regardless of character, background, or deservingness. The work produces the result.

> "Even if you don't deserve it in terms of your character, if you still do the actions — success does not care. Success is colorblind. Success only cares about the work that is done."

**Corollary:** Waiting to feel ready, or for circumstances to be fair, is a losing strategy. The actions are what the system responds to.

---

## 14. Advanced = Basics at Scale

**Model:** The difference between beginners and experts is not the complexity of what they do — it's the volume and consistency with which they do the basics.

> "Advanced is not doing the fancy stuff. It's doing more of the basics at scale."

> "Advanced people always do the basics."

**Application:** When growth stalls, check basics first: lead follow-up, conversion scripts, fulfillment quality, retention touchpoints. Complexity is rarely the answer.
